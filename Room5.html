Welcome to the women's room, where you'll discover a special knife that tells the story of Tulu women's strength and responsibility.

The Lady's Knife - Symbol of Power

This is the "Gejje Kathi" - a knife carried by Tulu women. But this wasn't just any knife for cutting vegetables! It was a symbol of a woman's role in protecting her family and managing her household.

Different Knives for Different Families

Just like the baby food grinders, the Gejje Kathi came in different versions:

  • Poor families: Simple iron knife

  • Middle-class families: Better quality iron

  • Rich families: Beautiful knives decorated with copper and silver!

But regardless of how fancy it was, every knife carried the same meaning: responsibility.

Ceremonial Uses

A woman receives her first Gejje Kathi during a special ceremony that happens when she attains puberty. After this she holds the knife during special ceremonies like her wedding or the birth of her child. This meant she was now fully responsible for her household and her child's safety.

From birth to death, Tulu women carried this knife as a sign of their strength. It showed that women in Tulu culture were protectors, decision-makers, and powerful members of society!
